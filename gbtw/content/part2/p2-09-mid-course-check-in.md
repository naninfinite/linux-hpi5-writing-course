**Part 2 --- Mid-Course Check-In**

*Week 6 Reflection: Halfway Through the Advanced Course*

Part 2 --- Doc P2-09

You are at the halfway point of Part 2. You should have a revised,
submission-ready short story, a second story either drafted or in
progress, and the beginning of a novel plan. This document is a
structured reflection --- not a test, not a performance. Its purpose is
to help you see clearly where you are, what has changed since Part 1,
and what the second half of Part 2 requires of you.

**Where Part 2's first half has taken you**

Phases 5 and 6 asked you to do something harder than writing a first
draft: to finish something. To take rough material through multiple
revision passes, to make the cuts you'd been avoiding, and to send the
result out into the world. Phase 6 then asked you to do it again --- a
second story, connected to the first, building on everything the first
story taught you about your own habits.

The second half of Part 2 is different in kind. Phases 7 and 8 ask you
to think at a scale you haven't worked at yet: the novel. That scale
change is not just practical --- it is psychological. A novel asks
something of a writer that a short story doesn't. This check-in is
partly about assessing your craft, and partly about assessing whether
you are ready for what comes next.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **If you have fallen behind:**

  Do not try to catch everything up before continuing. Take stock of
  where you actually are, set a realistic intention for the second half,
  and start from today. The most common reason writers abandon projects
  is the belief that falling behind has made completion impossible. It
  hasn't.
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

**Seven Reflection Questions**

Write your answers in full sentences. These are for you --- not to show
anyone, not to evaluate yourself against a standard. Honesty is the only
requirement.

**1. What is your short story's current state?**

Is it revised and submitted? Revised but not yet sent? Still in draft?
If it isn't submitted yet, what is the obstacle --- craft, fear, or
logistics? Name it specifically.

**2. What did the revision process teach you?**

Think about the four revision passes. Which was hardest and why? What
did you cut that you were reluctant to cut? Did the cuts improve the
story? What would you do differently in the next revision?

**3. What has your second story revealed?**

Writing a second story in the same world is different from writing the
first. What is easier? What habits from the first story are you
repeating --- deliberately or without noticing? What is the second story
doing that the first couldn't?

**4. How has your reading changed your writing?**

Le Guin, Jemisin, Hobb --- which writer has had the most visible effect
on your prose since you started reading them? Name one specific thing
you have tried to do differently because of what you read. Has it
worked?

**5. What is still consistently weak?**

Look at the list of craft elements from Part 1 and Part 2: told emotion,
dialogue subtext, scene structure, interiority depth, POV consistency,
sentence rhythm. Which one is still the problem you haven't solved? Be
specific --- not 'my dialogue' but 'my characters say what they mean
directly instead of talking around it.'

**6. Do you have a novel?**

Not a plan --- an idea. A character you need to spend 80,000 words with.
A world that has more in it than your short stories have shown. A
question you can't answer in 3,000 words. If yes, describe it in three
sentences. If not, write honestly about what's missing.

**7. What does the second half of Part 2 require of you that the first
half didn't?**

Phase 7 is novel planning. Phase 8 is novel writing. These require
stamina and a different relationship to not-knowing than the short story
phases did. What in you is ready for that? What in you is resisting it?

**Three Intentions for the Second Half**

Write three intentions for Phases 7 and 8. Not goals --- directions. A
goal is something you achieve or fail. An intention is something you
orient toward. Keep them specific and craft-focused.

Examples: 'I intend to write my novel's first chapter before planning
the rest of it.' 'I intend to apply the sequence map before drafting,
not after I've lost my way.' 'I intend to read McKee's Story
actively --- with a pen, not passively.'

**Your novel project --- a mid-point audit**

The second half of Part 2 is where the novel either takes hold or
stalls. Before you go further, answer these four questions about where
the project actually stands. These are not reflection questions ---
they're diagnostic. Be precise.

**1. What exists so far?**

List concretely: premise statement, protagonist sheet, world notes,
sequence map, opening pages. Not what should exist --- what does. If you
carried material forward from Part 1 (world sheet from module 15,
protagonist sheet from module 16), note how it has changed. A realistic
inventory is more useful than an optimistic one.

**2. What is the dramatic question of your novel?**

One sentence. Not the plot --- the question the story is testing. *Can a
person who has done terrible things earn a different kind of life? What
does loyalty cost when the cause is corrupt? How much of yourself can
you surrender before there is nothing left to return to?* If you can't
write it in one sentence yet, the novel doesn't have a spine. That's
fixable --- but you need to know it's missing before Phase 7 begins.

**3. What is the next scene?**

Not the next chapter. The next scene. Describe it in two sentences: who
is present, what happens, what changes. If you don't know, that is the
thing to solve before Phase 8 begins --- not by planning more, but by
writing toward it and seeing what the story tells you.

**4. What are you avoiding?**

Every novel in progress has a scene or a problem the writer circles
around rather than addressing. It's usually the most important thing in
the book --- the scene that changes everything, the confrontation that
costs most, the revelation that makes the rest of the story make sense.
Name yours. Then write it first.

**What the second half brings**

Phase 7 (Weeks 7--9) covers novel planning from first principles: the
premise, the sequence map, the first three chapters. Reading: McKee's
Story and Robin Hobb's Assassin's Quest --- the third Farseer book, in
which Hobb sustains a very long narrative through the sheer force of her
protagonist's interiority.

Phase 8 (Weeks 10--11) moves into sustained novel writing with advanced
craft: time control, the long middle, free indirect discourse, sub-plots
as structural reinforcement. Reading: Royal Assassin and The Art of
Fiction.

By Day 76 the expectation is: two revised short stories (at least one
submitted), three or more chapters of a novel, and a sequence map that
gives you a clear path forward.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **The novel truth:**

  Most writers who start a novel don't finish it. The ones who do have
  usually learned this: you don't finish a novel by knowing where it's
  going. You finish it by showing up and writing it even when you don't.
  The planning helps. The plan is not the thing.
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

*You are halfway through the advanced course. The second half is harder
and more rewarding. Keep going.*
