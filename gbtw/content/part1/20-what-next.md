**What Next --- After the Course**

*Where to Go from Here and How to Keep Going*

Part 1 --- Doc 20

You've finished the course. You have a writing habit, a body of work,
and at least one completed short story. This document is about what
comes after: how to sustain the practice, how to find your writing
community, where to go with your work, and how to think about the
longer-form writing that Part 2 of this course will take you into.

**The thing that ends and the thing that doesn't**

The course ends. The practice doesn't. The 84-day structure was
scaffolding --- it existed to build the habit and the craft foundations.
Now the scaffolding comes down and what's underneath needs to stand on
its own.

The most important thing to do immediately after finishing: keep
writing. Not necessarily every day --- but regularly, with intention.
The habit is fragile in the first few weeks after a structured programme
ends. Protect it.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **The minimum:**

  Three days a week, twenty minutes each. That's all it takes to keep
  the muscle from atrophying. More is better, but three days is the
  floor.
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

**Your completed short story --- what to do with it**

**Let it rest, then read it fresh**

If you haven't already done a full revision pass --- using the
four-pass system from doc 14 --- do that first. Give it at least a week
between the final draft and the final read. Then read it as a stranger
would: what works, what doesn't, what is genuinely earned.

**Find one reader**

Before submitting anywhere or sharing widely, find one reader you trust
--- not someone who will be automatically encouraging, but someone who
will read carefully and tell you honestly what they experienced. You're
not looking for line edits. You're looking for the answer to: did this
land? Where did you lose them? What confused them?

**Submitting short fiction**

If you want to submit your story for publication, here is the landscape:

-   Literary magazines --- both print and online. The Submission Grinder
    (thegrinder.diabolicalplots.com) is the best free database of short
    fiction markets. Filter by genre, word count, and payment level.

-   Genre magazines --- for dark fantasy specifically: Beneath Ceaseless
    Skies, Clarkesworld, The Dark Magazine, Nightmare Magazine, and
    Strange Horizons are well-regarded paying markets.

-   Anthology open calls --- publishers regularly open submissions for
    themed anthologies. Follow publishers like Tor, Solaris, and Titan
    Books on social media for announcement.

-   Start with magazines that accept simultaneous submissions. It takes
    months to hear back from most markets --- submitting to one at a
    time is unnecessarily slow.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  **On rejection:**

  Rejection is the default experience of short fiction submission. Even
  professional writers with extensive publication histories receive far
  more rejections than acceptances. A rejection is not a verdict on the
  quality of the work --- it's a mismatch between the work and what that
  editor needed at that moment. Keep submitting.
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

**Finding your writing community**

Writing is solitary. A writing community doesn't fix that --- but it
provides context, accountability, and the specific relief of being
around people who understand what the practice costs and what it gives.

**Writing groups**

A good writing group reads each other's work and responds with
craft-focused feedback. They are not cheerleading sessions and they are
not demolition derbies. The best ones have a clear format --- everyone
reads in advance, everyone comes prepared with specific feedback.

Finding one: local writing groups often advertise through libraries,
bookshops, and community centres. Online groups exist on Reddit
(r/fantasywriters, r/worldbuilding), Discord, and through genre
organisations.

**Genre organisations**

For dark fantasy and speculative fiction writers: the British Fantasy
Society (britishfantasysociety.org) and the Horror Writers Association
(horror.org) both have member communities, newsletters, and events. The
Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers Association (sfwa.org) has useful
resources even before you're eligible for full membership.

**Online communities**

Reddit's r/fantasywriters and r/worldbuilding are large, active, and
generally constructive. Discord servers around specific authors,
publishers, or genres can be valuable. NaNoWriMo (nanowrimo.org) has
year-round community infrastructure beyond its November event.

**Going to things**

Conventions and festivals --- FantasyCon, BristolCon, Nine Worlds in the
UK; World Fantasy Convention and various regional cons in the US --- are
where the genre community physically gathers. You don't need to be
published to attend. Going once is worth more than reading about it for
a year.

**Continuing to develop as a writer**

**What to read next**

The reading list in this course is a starting point. Beyond it:

-   The rest of Hobb's Farseer Trilogy: Royal Assassin and Assassin's
    Quest --- the arc completes across three books and the payoff is
    devastating.

-   The rest of Abercrombie's First Law world: Before They Are Hanged,
    Last Argument of Kings, and the standalone novels --- the thesis
    develops and sharpens.

-   N.K. Jemisin --- The Fifth Season (2015). The most formally
    innovative dark fantasy of the last decade. Remarkable POV choices
    and world-building.

-   Ursula K. Le Guin --- The Left Hand of Darkness and The
    Dispossessed. The gold standard for speculative fiction that is also
    serious literature.

-   More short story collections: read widely in the form you're
    writing. Kelly Link, Ted Chiang, Carmen Maria Machado.

**Craft books to read next**

-   Steering the Craft --- Ursula K. Le Guin. Short, precise, full of
    exercises. The best craft book for writers interested in prose
    style.

-   Story --- Robert McKee. Dense and demanding, but the most thorough
    treatment of narrative structure available.

-   The Art of Fiction --- John Gardner. Philosophical and rigorous. Not
    a quick read, but it will change how you think about what fiction is
    for.

-   Bird by Bird --- Anne Lamott. About the practice and the emotional
    experience of writing. A companion, not a manual.

**Part 2 --- What's coming**

Part 2 of this course picks up where Part 1 ends. Where Part 1 built
foundations, Part 2 builds structure --- longer-form work, completing

Part 2 covers: writing in series (connected short stories), planning a

It's structured as another 12-week daily course, building directly on
everything from Part 1. You should finish Part 1 before starting Part 2

**The novel question**

Before you cross into Part 2, there is one question worth sitting with.

You have built a world. You have built a protagonist. You have written a
story set in both. The question is not whether that story is finished ---
it's whether the world and the person in it have more to give.

Some short stories are complete. The world exists for the story, and
when the story ends, so does the world. Others are the visible surface
of something larger --- a place and a person who were already there
before the first scene started, and who will still be there after it
ends. Only you can tell which kind you have written.

If the answer is the second kind, that's your novel.

**Three questions before Part 2**

Write your answers before you close this document. A sentence or two is
enough --- these are for orientation, not performance.

**1. Does your protagonist have unfinished business?**

Not plot --- something deeper. A wound not yet resolved. A want they
haven't admitted to themselves. A version of the world they believe in
that the story hasn't finished testing. If yes, describe it in one
sentence.

**Your answer:**

**2. Does your world have pressure beyond the story you told?**

Political reality, history, a magic system with costs not yet explored,
a conflict that existed before your protagonist arrived and will
continue after they leave. If yes, what is the single most interesting
unexplored pressure in this world?

**Your answer:**

**3. What is the next thing that happens to this character?**

Not what you'd plan --- what you actually believe happens. Where are
they after the story ends? What do they want now? Write one paragraph.
It doesn't need to be good. It just needs to be honest.
