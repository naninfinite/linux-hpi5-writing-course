## Why this check-in matters

Part 4 is deliberately expansive. That is a strength, but it also
creates a risk: you may produce many interesting fragments without
deciding what genuinely matters. This check-in exists to stop that
drift.

The purpose of the mid-point is not to judge yourself harshly. It is to
become more accurate. By this stage, you should have enough material to
begin noticing where your energy rises, where your writing becomes thin,
what medium sharpens your strengths, what genre reveals a real emotional
or intellectual commitment, and which pieces deserve serious revision.

## When to do this check-in

Complete this document around Week 9, after you have worked through
cyberpunk, solarpunk, comics / graphic novels, at least some animation
work, and several prompts or cross-medium exercises.

## Part 1 --- Inventory of work completed

Begin by listing what you have actually produced so far.

### Speculative work

List cyberpunk exercises, cyberpunk scenes or openings, solarpunk
exercises, solarpunk scenes or openings, and any hybrid speculative
attempts.

### Visual storytelling work

List beat breakdowns, comics adaptation exercises, comics script drafts,
animation exercises, and animation scene or opening drafts.

### Project seeds

List any ideas that are not finished pieces but still feel promising.

## Part 2 --- What feels alive

Answer these as plainly as possible.

1\. Which speculative mode feels more natural to me: cyberpunk or
solarpunk?

2\. Which speculative mode challenged me in the most useful way?

3\. Which piece currently contains the strongest pressure?

4\. Which piece currently contains the strongest atmosphere?

5\. Which piece currently contains the strongest human centre?

## Part 3 --- Medium awareness

6\. Do I think most naturally in prose, in pages, or in movement?

7\. What did comics reveal about my prose habits?

8\. What did animation reveal about my pacing habits?

9\. Which visual medium feels most alive for me right now?

## Part 4 --- What is not working

10\. Which project is only interesting in theory?

11\. Where do I still become generic?

12\. What am I overusing?

13\. What am I avoiding?

## Part 5 --- Portfolio project audit

The final portfolio requires two completed pieces. By this point you
should have enough material to know which pieces are actually viable.
Answer these before deciding what to revise.

**1. What is your speculative piece?**

Name it specifically --- not *a cyberpunk story* but the actual premise
and protagonist. One sentence. If you can't name it yet, that is the
priority before anything else. A piece you can't describe in a sentence
isn't ready to revise --- it's still an idea.

**2. What is your visual storytelling piece?**

Same --- the actual script or sequence, not the form. What is it about,
who is in it, what does it do that prose couldn't? If you have two
candidates, name both and then choose one. Carrying two into the final
stretch means finishing neither properly.

**3. Which piece has the most life in it right now?**

Not which is most finished. Not which is most technically accomplished.
Which has the most energy when you work on it --- where does the writing
feel necessary rather than dutiful? That piece should receive the most
revision time in the final stretch. Energy in a draft is not a sign of
ease. It's a sign of genuine investment. Follow it.

**4. What needs to be cut or abandoned?**

Part 4 generates a lot of material. Not all of it belongs in the
portfolio. Name one thing you've been treating as a potential portfolio
piece that probably isn't --- and give yourself permission to let it
become a useful failure instead. Useful failures are not wasted work.
They are how you found out what you actually wanted to write. A clear
portfolio with two strong pieces is worth more than four pieces that
dilute each other.

## Part 6 --- Project decision

Choose one speculative piece to move forward and one visual storytelling
piece to move forward.

For each, write:

-   title or working title

-   genre mode or medium

-   current state

-   what it still needs most

You may also note one secondary piece that remains exploratory but
important.

## Part 7 --- Revision priorities

For your speculative piece, answer:

-   what is the clearest pressure in the piece?

-   where does the story lose force?

-   what must become more specific?

-   what should be cut?

-   what emotional effect do I want the final version to have?

For your visual piece, answer:

-   is the sequence clear?

-   where is the pacing weak?

-   what text can be removed?

-   where should the image or movement do more work?

-   what is the strongest beat, and how can I support it better?

## Part 8 --- Genre and medium direction

Write a short reflection on the following.

-   Which genre feels closest to my real writing instincts right now?

-   Which medium most changed the way I think?

-   What kind of future or pressure do I most want to keep exploring?

-   What do I want my final portfolio to say about me as a writer?

## Short project-priority template

Use this concise summary if useful.

-   Primary speculative piece

-   Primary visual piece

-   Secondary / optional piece

-   Biggest craft weakness to address

-   Biggest strength to preserve

-   What I will revise first

-   What I will stop overthinking

-   What I want the final portfolio to prove

## What a good Part 4 check-in sounds like

A useful check-in is specific, unsentimental, honest about weakness,
honest about excitement, and decisive enough to guide revision. It does
not need to sound grand. It only needs to tell the truth about where the
work is.

## Closing note

Part 4 becomes valuable when experimentation turns into direction. This
check-in is the moment where you stop asking *what could I do* and begin
asking *what do I most want to carry forward?* Answer that well, and the
final portfolio becomes much easier to build.
